This is our Week 3 task in Fundamentals of Computing Subject. It features an article about one of the top programming languages of today and additional information about Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) Models.


1. Python Programming Language

Python has gradually grown in popularity as one of the most widely used programming languages on the market. It is utilized in a variety of applications, including machine learning, web development, and software testing. It is suitable for both programmers and nonprogrammers.

Python is a programming language used to create websites and applications, automate operations, and perform data analysis. Python is a general-purpose programming language, which means it can be used to develop a wide range of programs and is not focused on any one issue. It has become one of the most widely used programming languages due to its versatility and beginner friendliness. It was the most popular programming language among developers in 2020, according to a survey done by industry analyst firm RedMonk.

Generally used for web and software development, task automation, data analysis, and data visualization, Python is adopted by many non-programmers such as accountants and scientists for different tasks. Examples include managing finances due to its relative ease of learning.

Python is used in many application domains: 1) Web and Internet Development, 2) Scientific and Numeric Computing, 3) Education, 4) Desktop GUIs, 5) Software Developments, and 6) Business Applications.


2. SDLC Models: Applications and Disadvantages
A) V Model

The V-model is an SDLC model that executes processes sequentially in a V-shape. The Verification and Validation model is another name for it. Based on a testing phase for each development stage, the V-Model is an extension to the waterfall model, which means a testing phase for every phase of the development cycle. After the previous step is completed, the following phase begins. It is a highly disciplined model. Testing and development phases are planned concurrently in the V-Model. In other words, there are Verification and Validation phases on either side of the V. There are two sides to every V-Model. The application of the V-Model is quite similar to that of the waterfall model, as both models are sequential. Before the commencement of a project, the requirements must be crystal clear, as it can be costly to make changes later on. Medical development uses this strategy because it is a highly disciplined industry. The V-Model technique has the advantage of being simple to learn and apply. This model's simplicity makes it easy to manage. Due to its inflexibility and high cost of updating the model in today's dynamic world, the model's drawback is that modifications are difficult and expensive to make.

The advantages are as follows:
• According to V-Model proponents, this system is highly disciplined, with each phase being completed in order.
• Ideal for smaller projects with clearly defined needs.
• Simple and easy to use.
• The model's stiffness makes it easy to manage. A review procedure and defined deliverables are part of each phase.

The following are some of the drawbacks of the V-Model method:
• Risk and unpredictability are high.
• For complex and object-oriented tasks, this paradigm is not suitable.
• Poor model for long-term and continuous initiatives.
• Not suitable for projects with a moderate to high risk of change in requirements, e.g.
• One cannot go back and change a feature of an application once it has been tested.
• At this point, no working code has been provided.

B) Big Bang Model

In the Big Bang model, we don't follow any particular process. Starting with money and effort as inputs results in software that may or may not meet client needs. This Big Bang Model does not follow a process/procedure, and there is very little planning required for this model to be effective and efficient. Even the customer is unsure of what he wants; the requirements are applied on the fly without many studies. This strategy is typically used for short projects with a small development team. This Big Bang Model has the virtue of being extremely simple and requiring little or no forethought. Manageable and without formal procedures. However, changes in needs or misinterpreted requirements might lead to a total reversal or scrapping of the project in the Big Bang Model. Ideal for minor, low-risk, repeated projects.

The Big Bang Model has the following advantages:
• In this case, we have a straightforward design.
• It's not necessary to plan ahead of time.
• Manageable
• Meager resource requirements
• Allows developers to be more creative
• Newcomers and students will benefit from it.

Below is a list of Big Bang Model drawbacks:
• Uncertainty and danger are high.
• For complex and object-oriented tasks, this paradigm is not suitable.
• Poor model for long-term and continuous initiatives.
• If requirements are misinterpreted, it can be pretty expensive.

C) Agile Model

Because of its focus on process adaptation, the agile SDLC model combines iterative and incremental processes to provide working software products quickly. Methods such as agile divide the product into small increments. Iterations of these builds are made available to users. Typically, each iteration lasts between one and three weeks. In every iteration, cross-functional teams work simultaneously on a variety of areas, including: 1) Analysis of Planning Needs; 2) Unit Testing and Acceptance Testing; 3) Unfinished prototypes are shown to customers as well as those who are involved in its development. According to the Agile model, it should handle every project differently. Existing methodologies should be customized to best suit the project requirements; to deliver specific features for a release. Tasks are separated into time boxes (short time frames) in Agile. The technique is iterative, with each iteration delivering a viable software build. In terms of features, each build is incremental; the final form contains all of the components required by the customer.

The following are the benefits of the Agile Model:
• Is a practical approach to software development.
• Encourages teamwork and cross-training.
• Functionality can be quickly developed and shown.
• The number of resources required is little.
• Suitable for fixed or changing needs
• Provides partly workable solutions as soon as possible.
• A good model for surroundings that change regularly.
• There are few regulations, and documentation is simple to implement.
• Allows for concurrent development and delivery within an overall planned context.
• There is little to no planning necessary.
• Simple to operate.
• Allows developers to be creative.

The following are the Agile Model's drawbacks:
• Not appropriate for dealing with intricate dependencies.
• There is a greater danger of sustainability, maintainability, and extensibility.
• It will not work unless there is an overall plan, an agile leader, and agile PM practices.
• Strict delivery management mandates the scope of the project, the functionality to be supplied, and the revisions needed to fulfill the deadlines.
• Depends greatly on client engagement, thus if the customer is unclear, the team may be led astray.
• Because there is little documentation developed, there is a high level of individual reliance.
• Due to a lack of documentation, transferring technology to new team members may be difficult.

D) RAD Model

Prototyping and iterative development are the cornerstones of the Rapid Application Development (RAD) approach. While writing the software, you'll need to think about how you will create a product. Focus is on acquiring customer requirements through workshops or focus groups, early prototyping by the customer using an iterative concept, reuse of existing components, continuous integration, and speedy delivery. The RAD model can be successfully applied to projects where unambiguous modularization is possible. RAD may fail if the project cannot be divided into modules. The instances in which RAD can be employed are described in the following:

1. When a system can be modularized and deployed incrementally, RAD should be employed.

2. It should be employed if there is a high availability of Modeling designers.

3. It should only be utilized if the budget allows for the usage of automated code generation technologies.

4. The RAD SDLC paradigm should be used only if domain specialists with relevant business knowledge are available.

5. Should be utilized when project requirements change and workable prototypes are to be given to the customer in minor iterations of 2-3 months.

The RAD paradigm provides rapid delivery by reducing overall development time due to component reusability and simultaneous development. RAD works well only when highly skilled engineers are available, and the customer is also committed to completing the targeted prototype within the time limit specified. The model may fail if either side is unwilling to commit.

The following are the benefits of the RAD Model:
• Changing needs can be addressed.
• Progress may be quantified.
• With the usage of sophisticated RAD tools, iteration time can be reduced.
• Productivity with fewer individuals in a shorter period of time.
• Development time has been cut in half.
• Increases component reusability.
• Initial reviews are conducted quickly.
• Encourages comments from customers.
• Integration from the start solves a lot of integration problems.

The following are the RAD Model's drawbacks:
• RAD can only be used to build systems that can be modularized.
• Highly talented developers/designers are required.
• High reliance on modeling abilities.
• Inapplicable to lower-cost projects due to the high expense of modeling and automated code development.
• Management complexity is increasing.
• Suitable for component-based and scalable systems.
• Throughout the life cycle, user interaction is required.
• Suitable for projects that require rapid development.
